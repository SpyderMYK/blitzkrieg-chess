How Blitzkrieg Works

You see only your own pieces. On each turn the referee tells you how many pawn tries (legal pawn captures) you have. Attempting a pawn try is optional, but capped at 3 attempts per turn; after that, any other move is fine. Captures are announced by square: “Pawn captured” (specific) or “Piece captured” (identity hidden). Illegal moves return “No”. Checks are announced by direction: knight, horizontal, vertical, or diagonal.

History & Acknowledgments

Kriegspiel (German for war game) is a blind chess variant invented by Michael Henry Temple in London in 1899. Two players sit at separate boards; a referee sees both and adjudicates, announcing only what each side is entitled to know — captures by square, checks by direction, and pawn tries. It became a staple of university chess and math clubs through the twentieth century for the way it turns chess into a game of deduction under uncertainty.

This version is built in tribute to Leston Newbill, who wrote one of the first electronic implementations of Kriegspiel at the University of Arizona in the early 1980s — long before the web, when a program that could arbitrate a blind chess game between two players on a shared system was a small marvel. Generations of students played it. Leston still maintains an online incarnation (without a computer opponent) at sencounter.net.

For Leston — thank you for the game.
